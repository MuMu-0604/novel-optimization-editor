# Dialogue Editing

Use this reference when the user asks about dialogue, character voice, subtext, argument scenes, confession scenes, interrogation scenes, relationship tension, or exposition-heavy speech.

## Dialogue Function Checklist

For each exchange, identify:

```text
Speaker:
Surface intent:
Hidden intent:
Power position:
Information revealed:
Information withheld:
Relationship movement:
Conflict pressure:
Voice markers:
Line-level issue:
Suggested edit:
Permission level:
```

## Checks

1. Does each speaker want something in the exchange?
2. Does the line change pressure, information, relationship, or emotional temperature?
3. Is the line saying what the character would actually say, not what the author needs explained?
4. Is there subtext where the relationship calls for it?
5. Does the speaker know the information they mention?
6. Does the diction match age, class, region, education, profession, era, genre, mood, and relationship?
7. Are action beats interrupting, escalating, hiding, or revealing emotion rather than decorating speech?
8. Can some exposition become conflict, misunderstanding, accusation, evasion, or bargaining?
9. Are repeated names, direct explanations, and complete sentences intentional?
10. Does silence do useful work?

## Common Dialogue Problems

| Problem | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Exposition mouthpiece | Character explains known facts | Convert to conflict, omission, or discovery |
| Same voice | Speakers sound interchangeable | Give each speaker vocabulary, rhythm, values, and avoidance patterns |
| No subtext | Characters say exact feelings too easily | Add denial, deflection, contradiction, or action beat |
| No turn | Conversation ends where it began | Add new information, decision, wound, threat, or concession |
| Over-tagging | Tags and beats slow every line | Use tags only for clarity, beats for pressure |
| Knowledge leak | Character references unknown info | Remove, delay, or add discovery |

## Revision Pattern

1. Mark what each character wants.
2. Cut lines that neither pursue nor resist a want.
3. Replace direct explanation with pressure when possible.
4. Preserve one or two voice-specific imperfections.
5. Recheck continuity and reveal order.
